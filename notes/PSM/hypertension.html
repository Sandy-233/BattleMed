🩺 Hypertension

Definition

Hypertension is a chronic condition in which blood pressure remains persistently above the normal range on repeated measurements. It is a major risk factor for heart disease, stroke and kidney disease.

Remember: Persistent elevation of blood pressure requires proper evaluation and long-term management.

Epidemiology

Feature Description
Global Burden One of the leading causes of cardiovascular disease.
Age Frequency increases with age.
Sex Seen in both males and females.
Importance Major preventable cause of heart disease, stroke and kidney disease.

Hypertension in India

  • Common in both urban and rural populations.
  • Many patients remain undiagnosed.
  • Lifestyle changes have increased prevalence.
  • Early screening is important.
  • Control rates remain lower than desired.

Diagnosis

Hypertension is diagnosed by measuring blood pressure accurately on more than one occasion using a validated blood pressure measuring device. A single elevated reading is not sufficient for diagnosis unless there is a hypertensive emergency.

Patient at Rest ↓ Correct Blood Pressure Measurement ↓ Repeat Measurement ↓ Average Readings ↓ Confirm Diagnosis

Blood Pressure Measurement

  • Patient should rest for at least 5 minutes.
  • Avoid tea, coffee, smoking and exercise for 30 minutes before measurement.
  • Patient should sit comfortably with back supported.
  • Feet should be flat on the floor.
  • Arm should be supported at heart level.
  • Use the correct cuff size.
  • Take at least two readings and calculate the average.
  • Measure blood pressure in both arms during the first visit.

Tracking of Blood Pressure

Regular monitoring helps assess disease progression, treatment response and long-term blood pressure control.

Method Purpose
Clinic Blood Pressure Routine diagnosis and follow-up.
Home Blood Pressure Monitoring Long-term monitoring and treatment compliance.
Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring Detects white-coat and masked hypertension.

Rule of Halves

100% Hypertensive Patients ↓ Only 50% Know They Have Hypertension ↓ Only 50% Receive Treatment ↓ Only 50% Achieve Adequate Blood Pressure Control

The Rule of Halves explains that many people with hypertension remain undiagnosed, many diagnosed patients are untreated, and among those treated only a proportion achieve adequate blood pressure control.

High Yield: The Rule of Halves highlights the importance of screening, early diagnosis and regular follow-up in hypertension control programmes.

Complications of Hypertension

Organ Complication
Heart Coronary artery disease, heart failure, left ventricular hypertrophy.
Brain Stroke, transient ischemic attack.
Kidney Chronic kidney disease.
Eye Hypertensive retinopathy.
Blood Vessels Peripheral arterial disease and aneurysm.

Target Organ Damage

Persistent Hypertension ↓ Damage to Blood Vessels ↓ Heart • Brain • Kidney • Retina ↓ Disability & Premature Death

Prevention and Control

Health Education ↓ Early Detection ↓ Lifestyle Modification ↓ Regular Blood Pressure Monitoring ↓ Treatment Compliance ↓ Prevent Complications

Hypertension can be effectively controlled through lifestyle modification, regular screening, early diagnosis, medication when indicated, and continuous follow-up.

DASH Diet

The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet is an eating pattern designed to reduce blood pressure and improve cardiovascular health.

Remember: DASH diet combined with regular exercise produces significant reduction in blood pressure.

Nutrition

  • Reduce daily salt intake.
  • Increase fruits and vegetables.
  • Eat fibre-rich foods.
  • Limit saturated and trans fats.
  • Reduce sugary beverages.
  • Maintain adequate potassium intake through diet.
  • Drink sufficient water.

Weight Control

  • Maintain healthy BMI.
  • Reduce abdominal obesity.
  • Exercise regularly.
  • Avoid sedentary lifestyle.
  • Balanced calorie intake.

Behavioural Changes

  • Stop smoking.
  • Limit alcohol intake.
  • Regular physical activity.
  • Stress management.
  • Adequate sleep.
  • Regular medical follow-up.
  • Medication adherence.
